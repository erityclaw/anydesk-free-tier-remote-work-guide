Decision guide

What to use instead, or what to do next

The best replacement depends on what kind of remote worker you are: solo employee, contractor, IT-heavy power user, or someone who only needs access to one office machine.

Best default recommendation

If you already depend on AnyDesk and the workflow is work-related, the lowest-friction move is usually to switch to a licensed remote access product instead of trying to stretch a personal tier beyond its intended use.

Option Best for Why it helps Tradeoff
Move to paid AnyDesk
Safest continuity		 People who want the same tool with fewer workflow surprises Better alignment with professional use, formal licensing, and business-grade features You start paying, but you stop fighting the licensing mismatch
RustDesk
Flexible		 Users who want more control or self-hosting potential Can be cost-effective and customizable, especially for technically comfortable users More setup and more responsibility
Chrome Remote Desktop
Simple		 Lightweight single-user access to a known machine Very low friction and good for straightforward access Less admin depth for serious business workflows
TeamViewer or RemotePC
Business-focused		 Workers or teams that need supportability and clearer commercial positioning Designed around ongoing remote work and support use cases Higher cost than a free tool
VPN + native remote tools
IT-led		 Employers with existing infrastructure Can align better with corporate security and endpoint policy Usually needs IT involvement and stronger device management

This is a practical fit comparison, not a ranking of absolute quality.

If you are a solo remote worker

Choose the path that gives you predictable access every day, even if it costs a little more.

If your employer cares about policy

Use a tool and licensing model that clearly fits commercial work. That conversation gets easier fast.

If budget is the only blocker

Measure the price against one failed workday. The math often changes immediately.

Recommendation: for real work, either license the tool you already rely on or move to a remote access product whose usage model clearly matches business use.
Good remote access should feel boring, stable, and compliant.
